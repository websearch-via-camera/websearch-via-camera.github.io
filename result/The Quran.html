The Quran

وَإِنَّ رَبَّكَ لَهُوَ الْعَزِيزُ الرَّحِيمُ

your Lord alone is the Almighty, the Merciful.

وَإِنَّهُۥ لَتَنزِيلُ رَبِّ ٱلْعَـٰلَمِينَ

Truly, this Quran has been sent down by the Lord of the Worlds:

زَلَ بِهِ ٱلرُّوحُ ٱلْأَمِينُ

the Trustworthy Spirit brought it down

عَلَىٰ قَلْبِكَ لِتَكُونَ مِنَ ٱلْمُنذِرِينَ

to your heart, so that you could bring warning

بِلِسَانٍ عَرَبِىٍّۢ مُّبِينٍۢ

in a clear Arabic tongue.

وَإِنَّهُۥ لَفِى زُبُرِ ٱلْأَوَّلِينَ

This was foretold in the scriptures of earlier religions.

أَوَلَمْ يَكُن لَّهُمْ ءَايَةً أَن يَعْلَمَهُۥ عُلَمَـٰٓؤُا۟ بَنِىٓ إِسْرَٰٓءِيلَ

Is it not proof enough for them that the learned men of the Children of Israel have recognized it?


These verses are from Surah Ash-Shu'ara, which means The Poets in English. This surah has 227 verses and is the 26th chapter of the Quran. It was revealed in Mecca, before the Prophet Muhammad's migration to Medina.

The verses you have entered are part of the introduction of the surah, which establishes the divine origin and authority of the Quran. The surah then narrates the stories of various prophets who faced rejection and opposition from their people, such as Moses, Abraham, Noah, Hud, Salih, Shu'ayb, and Jonah. The surah also mentions the punishments that befell the disbelievers and the rewards that awaited the believers. The surah ends with a warning to the Quraysh, the tribe of Mecca, who were hostile to the Prophet Muhammad and his message.

The surah is named The Poets because it contains a criticism of the pagan poets of Arabia, who used their eloquence to mislead people and mock the Quran. The surah asserts that the Quran is not the word of a poet, but the revelation of God, and that the poets will be accountable for their words on the Day of Judgment.

The surah is also known for its rhythmic and melodious style, which enhances its impact and beauty. The surah uses various literary devices, such as repetition, parallelism, contrast, metaphor, and onomatopoeia, to convey its meaning and emotion.
